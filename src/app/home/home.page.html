MVPxFFF Food Computer

About

MVP x FFF Food Computer is an AI-assisted thinking and tinkering tool for harmonizing the rhythms and culinary possibilities of Minimum Viable Proteins (MVPs) and Food Forest Flavours (FFFs).

The computer remixes ingredients based on harvest dates and user preferences, creating novel and delicious recipes that are ecologically-minded and nutritionally-complete.

Instead of trying to create one perfect approach to agriculture, this artistic research project uses artificial intelligence to graft together two visions of the future that are usually pitted against each other: alternative protein production and agroforestry.

But can high-tech, hyper-efficient proteins pair well with regenerative microseasonal ingredients in the kitchen? The MVP x FFF Food Computer connects these two complementary, but differently optimized, farming typologies through recipes and flavor.

Select your location and the date you want to eat, then add your preferences, and the MVP x FFF Food Computer will do the rest! But will it taste good?

What’s a Food Forest?

“A human-designed productive ecosystem, modeled on a natural forest with a high diversity of perennial and/or woody species, whose parts (fruits, seeds, leaves, stems, etc.) serve as food for humans.” — Defined by Green Deal Voedselbossen, NL

A food forest contains perennial plants, various layers of vegetation (tall trees, shrubs, groundcover, etc.), plant guilds or small groups of mutually beneficial species, and provide significant ecosystem benefits.

Food forests are agroforestry projects that take years to establish and emphasize biodiversity, resilience and regeneration. They create very different flavors throughout the year and across their lifetimes. Food forest products often can not be easily stored, transported or preserved.

Examples: Soft berries, delicate leaves, wild herbs, fresh mushrooms and small tubers.

Food Forest Opportunities: Resilient in the face of climate change. Provide many ecosystem services. Climate change resilient, biodiverse, carbon capturing.

Food Forest Risks: Slow start up time. Less efficient, lack automation, and are often not economically viable in our current system.

What’s an MVP?

MVPs: are non-animal protein sources that are attempting to minimize environmental harm as compared to conventional meat, dairy, and egg production. Some MVPs have centuries of use in human food systems (beans, legumes and nuts) while others have only been developed in recent decades. Often, MVPs use highly concentrated Controlled Environment Agriculture systems and take a technology-based approach to food production.

Examples: Plant-based meatballs, precision fermentation eggs, insect flour, algae-based seafoods.

MVP Opportunities: Hyper efficiency. Reduced water, energy and waste. Freeing up farm land for other ecological uses.

MVP Risks: No uptake. Often lack flavor, seasonality, and locality. Although they minimize harm, they often don’t add value or regenerate land.

Note: “Consuming more non- or minimally processed plant proteins like legumes and nuts and less meat, fish and dairy, is the easiest and often most sustainable way of restoring the protein balance.” — Future Protein NL.

Chestnut Berry Bites

## Chestnut Berry Bites: The Snack You Didn't Know You Needed!

### Ingredients

- 2 cups Frozen Sweet Chestnuts

- 1 cup Wildberry Jam

- 2 cups Oatly Outgurt (Natural)

- 1 cup Whole Wheat Flour

- 1 cup Rolled Oats

- 1/2 cup Chopped Fresh Herbs (parsley, chives, and mint)

- 1 cup Grated Carrot

- 1/2 cup Diced Red Bell Pepper

- 1/2 cup Diced Cucumber

- 1/2 cup Chopped Walnuts

- 1/4 cup Olive Oil

- 2 tbsp Lemon Juice

- Salt and Pepper to taste

### Instructions

1. **Prepping the Chestnuts:**

   - Boil the frozen sweet chestnuts in a pot of water for 10 minutes until tender. Drain and cool.

   - Once cooled, chop the chestnuts into small pieces.

2. **Making the Dough:**

   - In a large bowl, mix the whole wheat flour, rolled oats, chopped chestnuts, grated carrot, and chopped walnuts.

   - Add half the Oatly Outgurt and mix until it forms a dough-like consistency. If the mixture is too dry, add more Oatly Outgurt as needed.

3. **Shaping the Bites:**

   - Take small portions of the dough and roll them into bite-sized balls.

   - Flatten each ball slightly to form mini patties.

4. **Cooking the Patties:**

   - Heat olive oil in a large skillet over medium heat.

   - Cook the patties for 3-4 minutes on each side until golden brown and crispy. Remove from heat and let cool slightly.

5. **Topping and Assembly:**

   - In a small bowl, mix the remaining Oatly Outgurt with lemon juice, chopped fresh herbs, diced red bell pepper, and cucumber. Season with salt and pepper.

   - Spread a dollop of wildberry jam on each patty.

   - Top with a spoonful of the herbed Oatly Outgurt mixture.

### Prep and Cooking Time

- **Prep Time:** 20 minutes

- **Cooking Time:** 20 minutes

- **Total Time:** 40 minutes

### Servings

- **Serves:** 4 people
